Tailoring Institutional Professional Development Resources for Surgery Administrators

Michelle Fellenz
Learning and Development Manager
University of Wisconsin Medical Foundation

Shaun Hernandez
Surgery Outreach Program Manager
University of Wisconsin


Michelle Fellenz		  
Shaun Hernandez

Many organizations, including those in the healthcare industry, often lack the basic institutional infrastructure to ensure that their human capital is actively nurtured, developed, and strategically leveraged.

About a decade ago, UW Medical Foundation (UWMF) lacked precisely this infrastructure. While professional development for administrative staff was not actively discouraged, a philosophy of “on your own time and on your own dime” prevailed. It’s not difficult to understand this sentiment; after all, an hour spent refining “soft skills” and leadership behavior was a lost hour of actual work, and workers are paid to work, not to learn after all, right? Or so was the belief at the time.

Then UWMF experienced a significant cultural shift that placed a new focus on the optimization and development of human capital. The formation of the UWMF Learning and Development (L&D) program formalized these efforts. With foundational pillars of program development, leadership development, and training, L&D exists to reinforce and promote the mission, vision, values, and strategy of UWMF and the larger UW Health enterprise.

Although the work of L&D is wide and varied, permeating themes include communication, trust, team building, conflict resolution, change management, and service excellence. Popular tools used in many sessions facilitated by L&D are the DISC assessment and Belbin Team Inventory, behavioral assessments that aim to maximize the value of human capital at both the individual and team levels.

Like most organizations, health systems are not monolithic; they are comprised of many subunits with unique needs, goals, and cultures. Departments of surgery, for example, tend to be solution-oriented and fast-paced. Given this, opportunities for self-reflection and deliberation are a rarity despite their potential value.

In 2014, a small group of administrators from the UW Department of Surgery spent a half-day participating in an L&D professional development session. The majority of the time was spent completing and discussing individual DISC profiles, with a focus on understanding how each staff member derives value from work, responds to criticism, and more generally fits in as part of the overall team.

During the session, the opportunity to step outside our own four walls and work with L&D staff—still part of the larger UW Health organization but not entrenched in the particular culture of our department—was especially valuable. Upon completion of the session, the staff found themselves more sensitive to these nuances and better able to communicate effectively with all members of their team. In particular, by being more aware of how our colleagues intake and process information—particularly verbal communication—we are now better equipped to tailor our interactions so that they lead to intended results.

With even a small investment of staff time and energy, real rewards can be realized; we encourage you to take advantage of your institution’s professional development resources for the benefit of your departments as well.
